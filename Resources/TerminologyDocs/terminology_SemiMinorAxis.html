Semi Minor Axis

1. Geometrical Definition

The semi minor axis, commonly denoted by the symbol b, is a fundamental geometric parameter of an ellipse. An ellipse is defined as the locus of all points in a plane for which the sum of the distances to two fixed points, called the foci, is constant.

An ellipse possesses two mutually perpendicular principal axes:

  • Semi major axis (a): half of the longest diameter of the ellipse
  • Semi minor axis (b): half of the shortest diameter of the ellipse

The semi minor axis is oriented perpendicular to the semi major axis and passes through the center of the ellipse. By definition, its magnitude satisfies:

b ≤ a

2. Mathematical Formulation

2.1 Cartesian Equation of an Ellipse

For an ellipse centered at the origin and aligned with the coordinate axes, the standard Cartesian equation is:

(x² / a²) + (y² / b²) = 1

Here, the semi minor axis b determines the maximum extent of the ellipse along the axis orthogonal to the semi major axis.

2.2 Relation to Focal Distance and Eccentricity

Let c be the distance from the center of the ellipse to either focus. The geometric relationship between a, b, and c is given by:

c = √(a² − b²)

The eccentricity e of the ellipse is defined as:

e = c / a

Combining these relations yields a direct expression for the semi minor axis:

b = a √(1 − e²)

This equation demonstrates that the semi minor axis is uniquely determined by the semi major axis and the eccentricity.

3. Physical Significance in Celestial Mechanics

3.1 Keplerian Orbits

In classical celestial mechanics, the orbit of a body under a central inverse-square force (the two-body problem) is a conic section. For bound orbits, this conic section is an ellipse, with the central mass located at one focus.

In this context:

  • The semi major axis a defines the overall size and orbital energy
  • The semi minor axis b characterizes the transverse extent of the orbit

The semi minor axis is not an independent orbital element but is derived from a and e via:

b = a √(1 − e²)

3.2 Orbital Area and Kepler’s Second Law

The area A of an elliptical orbit is given by:

A = π a b

This area is directly related to Kepler’s second law, which states that a line connecting the orbiting body to the central mass sweeps out equal areas in equal times.

4. Limiting Cases and Interpretation

Eccentricity (e) Geometric Shape Behavior of the Semi Minor Axis
e = 0 Circle b = a
0 < e < 1 Ellipse b < a
e → 1 Highly elongated ellipse b → 0

5. Applications

The semi minor axis appears in numerous scientific and technical disciplines, including:

  • Astrodynamics and orbital mechanics
  • Geodesy and reference ellipsoids of planetary bodies
  • Physics of bound dynamical systems
  • Statistics (e.g., covariance and confidence ellipses)

6. Concise Scientific Definition

The semi minor axis is defined as half the length of the shorter principal axis of an ellipse. It represents the maximum radial extent of the ellipse perpendicular to the semi major axis and is mathematically expressed as:

b = a √(1 − e²)
