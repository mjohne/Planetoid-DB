Perihelion Distance

1. Conceptual Definition

The perihelion distance is a fundamental parameter in celestial mechanics that defines the minimum distance between an orbiting body and the Sun during one complete orbital revolution.

It corresponds to the point of closest approach of the body to the Sun and is a key descriptor of the size and shape of heliocentric orbits.

2. Perihelion and Orbital Geometry

For an object moving along a Keplerian orbit around the Sun:

  • The perihelion is the point of minimum heliocentric distance
  • The opposite point of maximum distance is the aphelion

The line connecting perihelion and aphelion is known as the line of apsides.

3. Mathematical Definition

For an elliptical orbit with semi major axis a and orbital eccentricity e, the perihelion distance q is given by:

q = a (1 − e)

This relation follows directly from the polar equation of a Keplerian orbit evaluated at true anomaly ν = 0.

4. Relation to Other Orbital Parameters

Using the linear eccentricity c, the perihelion distance can also be written as:

q = a − c

where:

  • a is the semi major axis
  • c = a e is the linear eccentricity

For a circular orbit (e = 0), the perihelion distance equals the orbital radius:

q = a

5. Applicability to Different Orbit Types

The perihelion distance is defined for all heliocentric Keplerian orbits:

Orbit Type Perihelion Distance
Elliptical (0 ≤ e < 1) Finite minimum distance
Parabolic (e = 1) Minimum distance equals semi latus rectum / 2
Hyperbolic (e > 1) Finite closest approach during flyby

6. Dynamical and Physical Significance

The perihelion distance strongly influences:

  • The maximum orbital velocity (via conservation of angular momentum)
  • Thermal and tidal effects experienced by the body
  • Solar radiation pressure and sublimation processes (for comets)

Objects with very small perihelion distances may experience intense heating and strong gravitational perturbations.

7. Relation to True Anomaly and Motion

The perihelion occurs at true anomaly:

ν = 0

At this point, the orbital speed reaches its maximum value, consistent with Kepler’s second law.

8. Applications

The perihelion distance is widely used in:

  • Planetary and small-body orbit catalogs (e.g., MPCORB)
  • Classification of comets and near-Sun objects
  • Mission trajectory design and flyby analysis
  • Long-term dynamical studies of the Solar System

9. Concise Scientific Definition

The perihelion distance is the minimum heliocentric distance of an orbiting body. It is given by q = a (1 − e) for elliptical orbits and represents the point of closest approach to the Sun along a Keplerian trajectory.
