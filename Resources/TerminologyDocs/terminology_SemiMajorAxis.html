Semi Major Axis

1. Geometrical Definition

The semi major axis, commonly denoted by the symbol a, is the most important geometric parameter of an ellipse. An ellipse is defined as the locus of all points in a plane for which the sum of the distances to two fixed points, known as the foci, is constant.

An ellipse possesses two mutually perpendicular principal axes:

  • Semi major axis (a): half of the longest diameter of the ellipse
  • Semi minor axis (b): half of the shortest diameter of the ellipse

The semi major axis lies along the direction of maximum extent of the ellipse, passes through its center, and connects the two vertices located farthest apart.

2. Mathematical Formulation

2.1 Cartesian Equation of an Ellipse

For an ellipse centered at the origin and aligned with the coordinate axes, the standard Cartesian equation is:

(x² / a²) + (y² / b²) = 1

In this representation, the semi major axis a determines the scale of the ellipse along its principal (long) direction.

2.2 Relation to Foci and Eccentricity

Let c denote the distance from the center of the ellipse to either focus. The fundamental geometric relationship between the ellipse parameters is:

a² = b² + c²

The eccentricity e is defined as the ratio:

e = c / a

Thus, the semi major axis may be expressed as:

a = c / e

for any non-circular ellipse (e ≠ 0). The semi major axis therefore governs the overall size of the ellipse, while the eccentricity controls its shape.

3. Physical Significance in Celestial Mechanics

3.1 Keplerian Orbits

In classical celestial mechanics, the motion of two bodies interacting via Newtonian gravitation results in a conic section. For bound systems, this conic section is an ellipse with the central mass located at one focus.

In this context, the semi major axis a is the most fundamental orbital element:

  • It determines the characteristic size of the orbit
  • It is directly related to the specific orbital energy
  • It controls the orbital period via Kepler’s third law

3.2 Orbital Energy and Period

For a body of negligible mass orbiting a central mass with standard gravitational parameter μ, the specific orbital energy ε is given by:

ε = − μ / (2a)

The orbital period T of a bound Keplerian orbit is:

T = 2π √(a³ / μ)

These relations highlight that the semi major axis uniquely determines both the energy state and the temporal scale of the orbit.

4. Limiting Cases and Interpretation

Eccentricity (e) Orbit Shape Interpretation of the Semi Major Axis
e = 0 Circle a equals the radius of the circle
0 < e < 1 Ellipse a defines the mean orbital size
e = 1 Parabola a → ∞ (marginally bound orbit)
e > 1 Hyperbola a is negative (unbound orbit)

5. Applications

The semi major axis is a central parameter in many scientific and engineering disciplines, including:

  • Orbital mechanics and astrodynamics
  • Planetary science and exoplanet characterization
  • Satellite mission design and trajectory analysis
  • Geodesy and reference ellipsoids

6. Concise Scientific Definition

The semi major axis is defined as half the length of the longest diameter of an ellipse. In gravitational two-body dynamics, it represents the fundamental measure of orbital size and is directly related to the system’s total mechanical energy and orbital period.
