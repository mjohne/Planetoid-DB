Directrix

1. Conceptual Definition

The directrix is a fundamental geometric concept associated with conic sections. It is a fixed straight line used, together with a focus, to define the shape and properties of a conic.

In both analytic geometry and celestial mechanics, the directrix provides an alternative but fully equivalent description of conic curves such as ellipses, parabolas, and hyperbolas.

2. Focus–Directrix Definition of Conic Sections

A conic section is defined as the set of all points for which the ratio of the distance to a fixed point (the focus) and the distance to a fixed line (the directrix) is constant.

|PF| / |PD| = e

where:

  • P is any point on the conic
  • F is the focus
  • D is the directrix
  • e is the eccentricity

3. Classification of Conics Using the Directrix

Eccentricity Conic Type
0 ≤ e < 1 Ellipse
e = 1 Parabola
e > 1 Hyperbola

Thus, the directrix plays a central role in the unifying geometric definition of all Keplerian orbits.

4. Directrix in Orbital Mechanics

In celestial mechanics, one focus of the orbit is occupied by the central gravitating body. The corresponding directrix is a hypothetical reference line that allows the orbital equation to be written in compact polar form:

r(ν) = p / (1 + e cos ν)

The directrix does not correspond to a physical object but provides a precise geometric reference associated with the orbit.

5. Geometric Interpretation

The directrix is always perpendicular to the major axis of the conic and located at a distance from the focus that depends on the eccentricity and focal parameter.

For an ellipse, the directrix lies outside the curve; for a hyperbola, it lies between the branches.

6. Relation to the Focal Parameter

The distance from the focus to the directrix is given by:

d = p / e

where p is the focal parameter (semi-latus rectum).

7. Physical Interpretation

Although the directrix has no physical manifestation in orbital motion, it provides a powerful conceptual tool for understanding how eccentricity controls the shape of an orbit.

It emphasizes that Keplerian orbits are purely geometric consequences of inverse-square central forces.

8. Applications

The directrix is used in:

  • Analytic geometry of conic sections
  • Derivations of Keplerian orbit equations
  • Educational explanations of eccentricity
  • Unifying treatments of bound and unbound orbits

9. Concise Scientific Definition

The directrix is a fixed straight line used in the focus–directrix definition of conic sections. A conic is the locus of points whose distance ratio to a focus and to the directrix is constant and equal to the eccentricity.
