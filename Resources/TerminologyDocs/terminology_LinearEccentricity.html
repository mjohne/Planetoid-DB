Linear Eccentricity

1. Conceptual Definition

Linear eccentricity is a fundamental geometric parameter of an ellipse and, by extension, of elliptic Keplerian orbits. It is defined as the linear distance between the center of the ellipse and either of its two foci.

The linear eccentricity provides a direct spatial measure of how far the foci are displaced from the geometric center and therefore quantifies the elongation of the ellipse in absolute (length) units.

2. Geometrical Definition

Consider an ellipse with:

  • Semi major axis: a
  • Semi minor axis: b

The linear eccentricity, commonly denoted by the symbol c, is given by:

c = √(a² − b²)

The two foci are therefore located at positions ±c along the major axis, measured from the center of the ellipse.

3. Relation to Orbital Eccentricity

The linear eccentricity must be distinguished from the orbital (dimensionless) eccentricity e. The two quantities are related by:

e = c / a

or equivalently:

c = a e

Thus, the linear eccentricity represents the dimensional counterpart of orbital eccentricity.

4. Interpretation in Celestial Mechanics

In celestial mechanics, the linear eccentricity describes the distance between the central gravitating body (placed at a focus) and the geometric center of the orbit.

For circular orbits (e = 0):

c = 0

indicating that the focus coincides with the center of the orbit.

As eccentricity increases, the focus moves farther away from the center, leading to more elongated orbits.

5. Relation to Periapsis and Apoapsis

Using the linear eccentricity, the distances to periapsis and apoapsis can be written as:

rperiapsis = a − c rapoapsis = a + c

These relations illustrate how linear eccentricity directly controls the asymmetry of orbital distances.

6. Extension to Other Conic Sections

While the concept of linear eccentricity is most commonly applied to ellipses, it can be generalized to hyperbolas, where c represents the distance from the center to each focus and satisfies:

c = √(a² + b²)

For parabolic trajectories, the concept of linear eccentricity is not defined, as a parabola has only one focus and no geometric center.

8. Applications

Linear eccentricity is used in:

  • Analytical geometry of conic sections
  • Orbital geometry interpretation
  • Derivation of periapsis and apoapsis distances
  • Educational and theoretical treatments of elliptical motion

9. Concise Scientific Definition

Linear eccentricity is the distance from the center of an ellipse to either focus. It is a dimensional measure of orbital elongation and is related to orbital eccentricity by the relation c = a e.
