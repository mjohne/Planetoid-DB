True Anomaly

1. Conceptual Definition

The true anomaly, commonly denoted by the symbol ν (Greek letter nu), is a fundamental angular parameter in celestial mechanics that specifies the instantaneous position of an orbiting body along its Keplerian orbit.

It is defined as the geometric angle between the direction of periapsis and the current position of the body, as measured at the primary focus of the orbit.

2. Geometrical Definition

Consider a body moving along a conic-section orbit with:

  • A central gravitating body located at a focus
  • A well-defined periapsis direction

The true anomaly is the angle:

  • Measured in the orbital plane
  • From the direction of periapsis
  • To the radius vector connecting the focus to the body
  • In the direction of motion
0 ≤ ν < 2π

3. Mathematical Representation

In polar coordinates with the origin at the focus, the equation of a Keplerian orbit is:

r(ν) = p / (1 + e cos ν)

where:

  • r is the radial distance
  • p is the semi latus rectum
  • e is the orbital eccentricity

The true anomaly directly determines the instantaneous distance from the central body.

4. Relation to Periapsis and Apoapsis

Special values of the true anomaly correspond to key orbital points:

True Anomaly ν Orbital Position
0 Periapsis (minimum distance)
π Apoapsis (maximum distance, elliptical orbits)

These positions define the line of apsides, which serves as the reference direction for measuring ν.

5. Relation to Eccentric and Mean Anomalies

The true anomaly is related to the eccentric anomaly E by:

tan(ν / 2) = √((1 + e) / (1 − e)) · tan(E / 2)

Through the eccentric anomaly, the true anomaly is linked to time via Kepler’s equation:

M = E − e sin E

where M is the mean anomaly.

6. Dynamical Interpretation

Unlike the mean anomaly, the true anomaly does not increase uniformly with time. Its rate of change reflects Kepler’s second law, according to which the orbiting body moves faster near periapsis and slower near apoapsis.

Thus, the true anomaly provides a physically intuitive description of orbital motion, directly corresponding to the actual angular position of the body.

7. Validity for Different Conic Sections

The true anomaly is defined for all Keplerian conic sections:

  • Elliptical orbits (0 ≤ e < 1)
  • Parabolic trajectories (e = 1)
  • Hyperbolic trajectories (e > 1)

In all cases, ν uniquely specifies the position of the body along the trajectory.

8. Applications

The true anomaly is essential in:

  • Instantaneous state vector computation
  • Orbit visualization and trajectory analysis
  • Astrodynamics simulations
  • Transformation between orbital elements and Cartesian coordinates

9. Concise Scientific Definition

The true anomaly is the angle, measured at the focus of an orbit, between the direction of periapsis and the radius vector to the orbiting body. It provides the direct geometric description of the body’s instantaneous position along a Keplerian trajectory.
