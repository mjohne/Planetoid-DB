Orbital Perimeter

1. Conceptual Definition

The orbital perimeter is a geometric quantity that describes the total path length of a closed orbital trajectory. In celestial mechanics, it most commonly refers to the circumference of an elliptical orbit.

Unlike the orbital period, which measures time, the orbital perimeter is a purely geometrical property with units of length.

2. Applicability and Terminology

The term orbital perimeter is not a standard Keplerian orbital element. It is a derived geometric quantity used primarily in analytical studies, numerical simulations, and comparative orbit geometry.

In formal orbital mechanics, quantities such as the semi major axis, eccentricity, and semi latus rectum are preferred over orbital perimeter because they allow closed-form dynamical relations.

3. Orbital Perimeter of an Elliptical Orbit

For a bound Keplerian orbit, the trajectory is an ellipse with:

  • Semi major axis a
  • Semi minor axis b

The orbital perimeter P is equal to the circumference of the ellipse.

4. Exact Mathematical Expression

The exact circumference of an ellipse cannot be expressed in elementary functions. It is given by an elliptic integral of the second kind:

P = 4a E(e)

where:

  • e is the orbital eccentricity
  • E(e) is the complete elliptic integral of the second kind

5. Approximate Formulae

Several accurate approximations are commonly used in practice. One of the most widely used is Ramanujan’s approximation:

P ≈ π [ 3(a + b) − √((3a + b)(a + 3b)) ]

For low eccentricity orbits, a simple approximation is:

P ≈ 2πa (1 − e² / 4)

6. Relation to Orbital Eccentricity

The orbital perimeter decreases with increasing eccentricity for a fixed semi major axis. In the circular limit (e = 0), the perimeter reduces to:

P = 2πa

which is the circumference of a circle with radius a.

7. Physical Interpretation

The orbital perimeter represents the total distance traveled by an orbiting body during one complete revolution. Combined with the orbital period, it can be used to define an average orbital speed:

vavg = P / T

This average speed has limited dynamical significance, as orbital velocity varies continuously along an elliptical orbit.

8. Limitations and Special Cases

For unbound trajectories (parabolic and hyperbolic orbits), the orbital perimeter is undefined, as the path length is infinite.

Thus, the concept of orbital perimeter applies exclusively to closed, bound orbits.

9. Applications

The orbital perimeter is occasionally used in:

  • Numerical orbit simulations
  • Comparative geometry of planetary orbits
  • Educational demonstrations of elliptical motion
  • Average velocity estimates in simplified models

10. Concise Scientific Definition

The orbital perimeter is the total geometric length of a closed orbital path. For bound Keplerian motion, it corresponds to the circumference of the orbital ellipse and can be expressed exactly using elliptic integrals or approximated by analytical formulae.
