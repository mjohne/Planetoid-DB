Mean Orbital Speed

1. Conceptual Definition

The mean orbital speed is a derived kinematic quantity in celestial mechanics that represents the average speed of an orbiting body over one complete orbital revolution.

It is defined as the ratio of the total path length of the orbit to the orbital period and must be distinguished from instantaneous orbital speed, which varies continuously along non-circular trajectories.

2. Formal Definition

For a bound Keplerian orbit, the mean orbital speed v̄ is defined as:

v̄ = P / T

where:

  • P is the orbital perimeter (total path length)
  • T is the orbital period

3. Circular Orbit as a Special Case

For a circular orbit of radius r, the orbital perimeter is 2πr and the orbital period is given by Kepler’s third law. The mean orbital speed therefore equals the instantaneous speed everywhere along the orbit:

v̄ = √( μ / r )

4. Elliptical Orbits

For elliptical orbits, the instantaneous orbital speed varies according to the vis-viva equation:

v = √( μ ( 2/r − 1/a ) )

The mean orbital speed, however, is obtained by averaging over the entire path and can be written as:

v̄ = (4a E(e)) / T

where E(e) is the complete elliptic integral of the second kind.

5. Approximate Expression

Using accurate approximations for the orbital perimeter, a commonly used approximation for mean orbital speed is:

v̄ ≈ 2πa (1 − e² / 4) / T

This approximation is valid for low to moderate eccentricities.

6. Relation to Other Orbital Speeds

Mean orbital speed must be clearly distinguished from:

  • Perihelion speed (maximum instantaneous speed)
  • Aphelion speed (minimum instantaneous speed)
  • Major orbital speed (characteristic speed at semi-major axis)
The mean orbital speed has no direct dynamical significance but is useful as a global average descriptor.

7. Physical Interpretation

The mean orbital speed represents the average distance traveled per unit time over one orbit. It does not obey simple conservation laws and does not uniquely characterize the orbital dynamics.

Nevertheless, it provides a convenient scalar measure for comparing different orbits.

8. Applicability and Limitations

Mean orbital speed is defined only for:

  • Closed, bound orbits (elliptical or circular)
For parabolic and hyperbolic trajectories, the mean orbital speed is undefined, as no finite orbital period or perimeter exists.

9. Applications

Mean orbital speed is used in:

  • Educational treatments of orbital motion
  • Comparative analysis of orbital trajectories
  • Simplified modeling and visualization
  • Order-of-magnitude estimates of orbital motion

10. Concise Scientific Definition

The mean orbital speed is the total length of a closed orbital path divided by the orbital period. It represents the average speed of an orbiting body over one full revolution and is a derived, non-fundamental quantity in orbital mechanics.
