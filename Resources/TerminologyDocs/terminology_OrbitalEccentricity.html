Orbital Eccentricity

1. Conceptual Definition

Orbital eccentricity, commonly denoted by the symbol e, is a fundamental, dimensionless parameter that characterizes the shape of an orbit in classical celestial mechanics. It quantifies the degree to which an orbit deviates from a perfect circle.

Orbital eccentricity is one of the six classical Keplerian orbital elements and applies to all conic-section trajectories resulting from motion under an inverse-square central force.

2. Geometrical Interpretation

Geometrically, the eccentricity of a conic section is defined as the ratio of:

  • The distance between a point on the conic and a focus
  • The distance between the same point and the corresponding directrix

For an ellipse with semi major axis a, semi minor axis b, and focal distance c:

e = c / a

where:

  • c = √(a² − b²)
  • 0 ≤ e < 1

3. Classification of Orbits by Eccentricity

Eccentricity Orbit Type Geometrical Shape
e = 0 Circular Circle
0 < e < 1 Elliptical Ellipse
e = 1 Parabolic Parabola
e > 1 Hyperbolic Hyperbola

4. Mathematical Formulation in Orbital Mechanics

In polar coordinates, with the origin at a focus, the equation of a Keplerian orbit is:

r(ν) = p / (1 + e cos ν)

where:

  • r is the radial distance
  • ν is the true anomaly
  • p is the semi latus rectum

The eccentricity determines how strongly the radial distance varies with the true anomaly.

5. Dynamical Definition

From a dynamical perspective, orbital eccentricity can be expressed in terms of the orbital energy and angular momentum. The eccentricity vector e⃗ is defined as:

e⃗ = (v × h) / μ − r̂

where:

  • v is the velocity vector
  • h is the specific angular momentum vector
  • μ is the standard gravitational parameter
  • r̂ is the radial unit vector

The magnitude of this vector equals the scalar eccentricity:

e = |e⃗|

6. Physical Interpretation

Orbital eccentricity governs the ratio between periapsis and apoapsis distances:

rperiapsis = a (1 − e) rapoapsis = a (1 + e)

As eccentricity increases, the orbit becomes more elongated, leading to stronger variations in orbital velocity and distance from the central body.

7. Special and Limiting Cases

For circular orbits (e = 0), the orientation of periapsis is undefined, and several orbital elements become degenerate.

For parabolic and hyperbolic trajectories, eccentricity determines whether the orbit is marginally bound or unbound.

8. Applications

Orbital eccentricity is a key parameter in:

  • Planetary, asteroid, and cometary orbit catalogs
  • Mission analysis and trajectory design
  • Long-term dynamical stability studies
  • Astrophysics and exoplanet characterization

9. Concise Scientific Definition

Orbital eccentricity is a dimensionless parameter that defines the shape of an orbit. It measures the deviation of a trajectory from circularity and uniquely classifies Keplerian motion into circular, elliptical, parabolic, or hyperbolic regimes.
